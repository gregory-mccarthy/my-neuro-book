# Nervous System

## Central Nervous System (CNS)

### Brain

#### Integrates body-state signals

#### Predicts needs and coordinates allostasis

### Spinal cord

#### Main highway between brain and body

#### Dorsal side: sensory input arriving

#### Ventral side: motor output exiting

## Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

### Big idea: the interface between CNS and body

#### Afferent = body → CNS

#### Efferent = CNS → body

#### Regulation is the loop, not a one-way command

### Somatic nervous system

#### Main job: body–world interaction

##### External sensation and skeletal-muscle control

#### Somatic sensory / afferent

##### Skin: touch, temperature, pain

##### Muscles and joints: proprioception

##### Cell bodies in dorsal root ganglia

##### Enters spinal cord through dorsal roots

#### Somatic motor / efferent

##### Skeletal muscle

##### Cell bodies in ventral horn of spinal cord

##### Exits spinal cord through ventral roots

##### Voluntary movement, reflexes, posture, and breathing muscles

##### Neuromuscular junction: acetylcholine → nicotinic receptors

### Autonomic / visceral nervous system

#### Main job: body–body regulation

##### Smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, glands, viscera, blood vessels

##### Usually unconscious, but never inactive

##### Continuous push-pull regulation of homeostasis and allostasis

#### Afferent side: the body reports up

##### Baroreceptors: blood pressure

##### Chemoreceptors: blood gases, pH, metabolites

##### Stretch receptors: gut, bladder, lungs, vessels

##### Nociceptors and irritant receptors: visceral pain, nausea, airway defense

##### Major routes: vagus, glossopharyngeal, and spinal visceral afferents

##### Vagal afferents land mainly in the nucleus of the solitary tract (NTS)

##### Visceral afferents do not map neatly onto sympathetic vs parasympathetic labels

#### Efferent side: the CNS acts back

##### Sympathetic and parasympathetic outputs tune many of the same organs

##### Opposition is usually complementary control, not a simple on/off switch

##### Real behaviors often co-activate both divisions

###### Sexual response: parasympathetic erection/arousal; sympathetic emission/ejaculation

###### Standing up: sympathetic vascular tone prevents a blood-pressure crash

###### Breathing: central rhythm plus autonomic airway and cardiac adjustments

#### Teaching slogans

##### Sympathetic: “fight or flight” = useful mnemonic, not a definition

##### Parasympathetic: “rest and digest” / “feed and breed” = useful mnemonic, not a definition

##### Better framing: both branches continuously tune controlled variables

#### Sympathetic division

##### Functional theme: mobilization and redistribution

###### Prepares the body for exertion, threat, cold, standing, attention, and metabolic demand

###### Also maintains baseline vascular tone and thermoregulation

##### Anatomy: thoracolumbar outflow

###### Preganglionic cell bodies in lateral horn / intermediolateral column, T1–L2/3

###### Exit via ventral roots → spinal nerves → white rami communicantes

###### Sympathetic chain / paravertebral ganglia

###### Prevertebral ganglia: celiac, superior mesenteric, inferior mesenteric, etc.

###### Adrenal medulla: modified postganglionic target releasing epinephrine and norepinephrine into blood

##### Neurochemistry

###### Preganglionic neurons: acetylcholine → nicotinic receptors

###### Most postganglionic neurons: norepinephrine → adrenergic receptors

###### Important exception: many sweat glands are sympathetically cholinergic

##### Common acute effects

###### Eye: pupil dilation and ciliary relaxation for distance vision

###### Heart: increased rate and contractility

###### Lungs: bronchodilation and easier airflow

###### Blood vessels: constricts skin/gut; active muscle and heart flow depend on local metabolism and receptor mix

###### Skin: sweating for thermoregulation; piloerection

###### Gut: usually reduces motility and secretion during acute exertion

###### Bladder: relaxes detrusor; contracts internal sphincter

###### Reproductive system: emission/ejaculation and orgasm-related autonomic patterns

###### Immune organs and marrow: regulates immune-cell trafficking and production

##### Chronic sympathetic load

###### Acute mobilization is adaptive; sustained mobilization can become costly

###### Sleep disruption

###### Appetite and weight dysregulation

###### Constipation or other GI dysmotility

###### Fertility and reproductive suppression

###### Cardiovascular strain and atherosclerotic risk

###### Immune and inflammatory modulation

#### Parasympathetic division

##### Functional theme: maintenance, recovery, and local organ support

###### Promotes digestion, secretion, elimination, sexual arousal, and cardiac slowing

###### Not simply “calm”; it is active in many coordinated behaviors

##### Anatomy: craniosacral outflow

###### Brainstem nuclei of cranial nerves III, VII, IX, X

###### Sacral spinal cord S2–S4

###### Ganglia usually near or within target organs

##### Cranial routes

###### CN III → ciliary ganglion → pupil constriction and ciliary muscle contraction

###### CN VII → pterygopalatine and submandibular ganglia → lacrimal, nasal, and salivary glands

###### CN IX → otic ganglion → parotid salivary gland

###### CN X / vagus → heart, lungs, and much of the gastrointestinal tract

##### Sacral routes

###### Pelvic splanchnic nerves S2–S4 → distal colon, rectum, bladder, and reproductive organs

##### Neurochemistry

###### Preganglionic neurons: acetylcholine → nicotinic receptors

###### Postganglionic neurons: acetylcholine → muscarinic receptors

##### Common effects

###### Eye: pupil constriction and near vision

###### Heart: decreased heart rate and slowed AV-node conduction

###### Lungs: bronchoconstriction and airway-tone modulation

###### Gut: increased motility, secretion, and digestion

###### Salivary, lacrimal, and nasal glands: increased secretion

###### Bladder: contracts detrusor; relaxes internal sphincter

###### Reproductive system: erection and sexual arousal

###### Immune system: vagal / cholinergic anti-inflammatory reflex

##### Vagus nerve: special emphasis

###### Longest cranial nerve; major parasympathetic route to thorax and abdomen

###### Mostly sensory: the body reports upward through the vagus far more than the brain commands downward

###### Afferent target: NTS → parabrachial nucleus, hypothalamus, limbic and forebrain pathways

#### Enteric nervous system (ENS)

##### Classification note

###### Often taught as the third division of the autonomic nervous system

###### Can also be displayed as its own PNS branch because local reflex circuits can run without direct CNS instruction

###### Best working phrase: intrinsic gut nervous system, modulated by sympathetic and parasympathetic inputs

##### Main job: local control of the gastrointestinal tract

###### The gut is not a passive target of brain commands

###### It senses, integrates, and acts locally

##### Location and organization

###### Embedded in the wall of the GI tract, from esophagus to rectum

###### Myenteric / Auerbach plexus: between longitudinal and circular muscle; major role in motility and peristalsis

###### Submucosal / Meissner plexus: in the submucosa; major role in secretion, absorption, and local blood flow

###### Hundreds of millions of neurons, roughly spinal-cord scale

##### Cell types and circuits

###### Intrinsic primary afferent neurons detect stretch and chemistry

###### Interneurons integrate local information

###### Motor neurons act on smooth muscle, glands, and blood vessels

###### Enteric glia support neurons and participate in barrier, immune, and motility functions

##### Functions

###### Motility: peristalsis, segmentation, sphincter coordination

###### Secretion: gastric, intestinal, pancreatic, and biliary coordination

###### Absorption and fluid/electrolyte exchange

###### Local blood-flow regulation

###### Enteroendocrine signaling

###### Entero-enteric reflexes: one gut region changes activity in another

###### Defensive GI responses: diarrhea/expulsion; vomiting uses gut signals plus brainstem circuits

##### Communication with the CNS and body

###### Parasympathetic modulation: vagus and pelvic splanchnic nerves

###### Sympathetic modulation: prevertebral ganglia and splanchnic pathways

###### Vagal and spinal afferents report gut state to the brainstem and beyond

###### Hormonal, immune, and microbial-metabolic channels also contribute to the gut–brain axis

##### Teaching caution

###### “Second brain” means local sensing, integration, and motor output

###### It does not mean the gut literally thinks, feels, or controls mood in a simple way
