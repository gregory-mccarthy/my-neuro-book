Primate relationships and the human lineage

A compact teaching chart showing where humans sit among monkeys and apes, and a zoomed view of selected extinct hominins. The human path is emphasized, but the figure deliberately avoids implying that evolution is a ladder.

Terminology note. In current usage, hominid means a member of Hominidae — great apes, including orangutans, gorillas, chimpanzees, bonobos, and humans. The extinct species on the human side after the chimpanzee/human split are more precisely called hominins. Dates below are rounded teaching estimates.

Panel A is a ladderized clade centered on the human line. It is useful for students because it makes clear that humans are apes, apes are catarrhine primates, and monkeys are not ancestors of humans.

Panel B is not a complete species inventory. It emphasizes widely taught taxa and fossil ranges that help students see overlap: several hominin species coexisted, and many lineages were side branches.

Source notes for revision

This chart uses rounded branch dates and fossil ranges synthesized from major teaching and research sources: Smithsonian Human Origins species pages; NCBI taxonomy browser for the broad taxonomic nesting; recent molecular timetrees of primates; comparative ape genome papers; and recent paleoanthropological updates on early hominins and Paranthropus. Branch dates and exact species relationships should be treated as approximate, because fossil assignments, mutation-rate assumptions, and incomplete lineage sorting can shift estimates.

Representative sources: Smithsonian Human Origins (early human species); NCBI Taxonomy (Hominidae, Homininae); Roos et al. / modern primate timetree syntheses; Carbone et al. 2014 on gibbons; Scally et al. 2012 and complete ape genome work on great ape splits; Hawks 2023 on early hominins; and recent Nature work on Paranthropus in Afar.
