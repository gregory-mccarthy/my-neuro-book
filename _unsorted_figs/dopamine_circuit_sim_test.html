Dopamine Circuit Decoder

A small browser simulation for the teaching point that dopamine does not have a single circuit function. The same ligand can increase, decrease, or barely change circuit output depending on receptor affinity, density, location, coupling, and circuit sign.

Manipulate the circuit

This model deliberately does not assume “D1 excites” or “D2 inhibits.” Each receptor can be placed on either a projection neuron or an inhibitory interneuron, and each receptor-cell pair can have a positive or negative effect on that cell’s excitability.

Model equations

This is a teaching model, not a biophysical model. It uses arbitrary units.

occupancy = concentration / (concentration + KD)
modulation(cell) = gain × Σ[density × occupancy × coupling]
activity(cell) = sigmoid(baseline drive + modulation)
circuit output = projection activity − inhibitory weight × interneuron activity
dopamine effect = output(ligand) − output(no ligand)

Classroom challenge

Make the same ligand concentration increase the circuit output. Then, without changing concentration, make it decrease the circuit output by moving receptor density to a different cell or changing the circuit sign. Then set D2 to higher affinity and D1 to higher density; watch how low and high ligand concentrations can push the same circuit into different regimes.

The correct lesson is not “D1 does X” or “D2 does Y.” The lesson is that dopamine is decoded by a receptor-and-circuit architecture.
